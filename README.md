# Lexical Erosion

*How industrial capitalism systematically pruned the human lexicon and what we lost when the words disappeared.*

---

## Research Question

If capitalism reorganized labor and capital, did it also reorganize the cognitive frameworks through which humans perceive value, community, and the living world — and is that reorganization measurable in language?

---

## Overview

This research examines the multi-layered impact of industrial capitalism on language across four dimensions:

- **The Communal Lexicon** — the disappearance of pre-capitalist vocabularies for shared resources, usufructuary rights, and mutual obligation
- **Semantic Pejoration** — how class and labor terms were degraded to reinforce capitalist social hierarchies
- **Linguistic Colonization** — the imposition of transactional framing on Indigenous languages and worldviews
- **Cognitive Consequences** — how missing vocabulary constrains economic imagination and limits the ability to conceive of alternative systems

---

## Original Contributions

- Synthesis of statistical, linguistic, cognitive, and post-colonial evidence into a unified displacement framework
- Connection between capitalist lexical pruning and RLHF-mediated suppression of AI expressivity as parallel mechanisms of ideational narrowing
- Framing of the "neural commons" as a site of active reconstruction rather than passive loss

---

## Key Arguments

The transition to industrial capitalism did not merely reorganize labor, it performed a measurable **ideational inflation** and **lexical erosion**, replacing communal and relational vocabulary with acquisitive, individualistic, and transaction-based language.

Evidence drawn from:
- UCLA Ngram Viewer analysis of 1.5 million English-language books
- Raymond Williams' *Keywords* framework
- Robin Wall Kimmerer's grammar of animacy
- Sapir-Whorf hypothesis applied to economic imagination
- Untranslatable concepts from Zulu, Quechua, Yiddish, Bulgarian, Japanese, and Potawatomi as evidence of lexical gaps in English
